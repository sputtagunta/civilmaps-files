üìñ Case Background

The Civil Maps Asset Sale

In 2022, Solfice Research, Inc. (d/b/a Civil Maps), an autonomous vehicle mapping technology company, sold substantially all of its assets to Luminar Technologies, Inc. through a transaction known as "Project Condor." The sale was approved by what appeared to be a 54% stockholder vote in favor of the transaction. However, according to the plaintiff's allegations, significant disclosure and process gaps may reduce the effective "cleansed" vote below the required 51% majority threshold, potentially affecting the transaction's validity under Delaware law.

Transaction Structure & Key Decision Makers

The transaction was structured as an asset sale from Solfice into Condor Acquisition Sub I and Condor Acquisition Sub II, entities associated with Luminar Technologies. Key decision makers included Austin Russell (Luminar's CEO and founder) and Thomas Fennimore (representing Luminar's interests in the transaction). On the Solfice side, the board of directors oversaw the approval process, though plaintiffs allege no special committee was formed to independently evaluate the transaction for conflicts of interest.

Compensation to Solfice Insiders

As part of the transaction, Luminar provided various forms of compensation to certain Solfice insiders, including:

  • Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) in Luminar Technologies
  • Release Agreements providing separation benefits
  • Employment agreements and other compensation arrangements

Key insiders who received compensation include Stefan Safko, Scott Harvey, and Satya Vakkaleri. Critically, both Stefan Safko and Scott Harvey served as board members with fiduciary duties to all stockholders. According to Section 7.2(e) of the Asset Purchase Agreement, the employment agreements for Scott Harvey and Stefan Safko were explicitly listed as closing conditions, demonstrating their compensation was integral to the transaction's completion.

Delaware Law: Silence as Admission

Under Delaware law and federal evidence principles, when a party is confronted with a specific factual assertion and has both the knowledge and opportunity to correct it but chooses not to, that silence can create a credible basis to assume the assertion is accurate. This principle is particularly applicable in adversarial contexts where:

  • The party has a strong incentive to deny false allegations
  • The party has access to the underlying facts
  • The assertion is specific and unambiguous
  • The party explicitly acknowledges receipt of the assertion but declines to contest it
Legal Precedent: Courts recognize that in civil litigation, a party's failure to deny specific factual allegations when directly confronted‚Äîespecially allegations within that party's unique knowledge‚Äîmay be treated as an implied admission or, at minimum, removes any evidentiary barrier to accepting the allegation as credible. See Wright v. Washington, Baxter v. Palmigiano, 425 U.S. 308 (1976) (silence can support adverse inference); Delaware Rules of Evidence 801(d)(2) (adoptive admissions).
Application to Chraim Compensation

Here's why Luminar's silence is significant:

  1. Specific assertion: Sravan didn't say "maybe" or "might have"‚Äîhe stated flatly that Chraim "received buyer-linked consideration at closing."
  2. Direct challenge: Sravan explicitly invited Luminar to correct the record if the assertion was false.
  3. Repeated refusal: Even after clarification was requested, Luminar counsel maintained their "no position" stance.
  4. Unique knowledge: Only Luminar knows who received closing-contingent compensation. If the assertion were false, Luminar could easily deny it.
  5. Strong incentive to deny: If Chraim didn't receive compensation, his 16% "yes" vote would remain valid and count toward the 51% threshold. Luminar's silence effectively concedes this crucial vote should be excluded.
Conclusion: Luminar's refusal to deny Chraim's compensation, despite multiple opportunities and strong incentive to do so, provides a credible evidentiary basis to treat the compensation as factual. Combined with Harvey and Safko's documented interested status (APA ¬ß 7.2(e)), this reduces the "disinterested" vote below the 51% threshold required by ¬ß 271.

Delaware Law: Vote Coercion and Corwin

Under Delaware law, for a stockholder vote to provide Corwin cleansing, it must be obtained from a majority of fully informed and uncoerced stockholders. Corwin v. KKR Fin. Holdings LLC, 125 A.3d 304 (Del. 2015). Vote coercion‚Äîthreatening economic harm to induce stockholder approval‚Äîinvalidates the cleansing effect.

Legal Standard for Coercion: A vote is coerced when a stockholder is presented with a threat of economic harm contingent on how they vote, such that they cannot make a free and voluntary choice. In re Pure Res., Inc., S'holders Litig., 808 A.2d 421 (Del. Ch. 2002); Solomon v. Armstrong, 747 A.2d 1098 (Del. Ch. 1999).
Application: The Coercive Choice Imposed on Common Stockholders

What Arman Pahlavan's email reveals:

  1. The Threat: The buyer cut the valuation from $20M to $10M‚Äîa 50% reduction in value‚Äîpartly because Sravan refused to commit his vote without seeing final transaction documents.
  2. The Impossible Choice: Common stockholders were told: (a) Sign now without full disclosure and get $20M, or (b) Demand disclosure first and get only $10M. This is coercion by definition.
  3. Blind Voting Demand: The buyer wanted a 90% vote before preparing final documentation. Sravan insisted on seeing "final dox" before voting. The buyer's response: cut the deal value in half.
  4. Exploitation of Desperation: Counsel admitted: "The company did not have money to operate and had to sell. Buyer knew that the company had gotten to desperation." This creates economic duress.
  5. Blaming the Victim: Counsel characterized Sravan's request for disclosure as "inability to cooperate" and "non-cooperation"‚Äîframing a reasonable demand for information as obstruction.
Conclusion: Cutting a transaction's value in half because a stockholder demands disclosure before voting is quintessential vote coercion. Stockholders cannot make a "free and voluntary" choice when threatened with a 50% value reduction for exercising their right to full information. This eliminates any Corwin cleansing effect the vote might otherwise have had.

The Corwin Requirements: All Three Must Be Satisfied

For a stockholder vote to cleanse a transaction under Corwin, three conditions must be met:

1. Fully Informed Vote

Required: Stockholders must receive full disclosure of all material facts.

Here: ‚ùå Board member employment agreements (APA ¬ß7.2(e)), RSU awards, and Chraim compensation allegedly not disclosed.

2. Uncoerced Vote

Required: Vote must be freely and voluntarily given without threats or economic duress.

Here: ‚ùå Valuation cut 50% (from $20M to $10M) after Sravan demanded disclosure before voting.

3. Disinterested Majority

Required: Majority of shares voting must be held by disinterested stockholders.

Here: ‚ùå After excluding Safko, Harvey, and Chraim (all allegedly received undisclosed compensation), vote allegedly falls below 51%.

Corwin Failure: All Three Prongs Fail

Result: Even if the stockholder vote passed with the reported 54% approval, it cannot provide Corwin cleansing because:

  • Stockholders were not fully informed (material compensation to board members and Chraim not disclosed)
  • The vote was coerced (valuation cut in half to punish disclosure demands)
  • The majority was not disinterested (board members and major stockholder received undisclosed benefits)

Without Corwin protection, the transaction is subject to entire fairness review, and Luminar faces potential rescissory damages, disgorgement, or other equitable remedies.

Delaware Law Framework

This case involves several key provisions of Delaware corporate law:

8 Del. C. ¬ß 271 - Sale of Assets

Requires approval by the board of directors and a majority of outstanding stockholder votes for a corporation to sell substantially all of its assets. This section establishes the fundamental requirement that asset sales of this magnitude must receive stockholder approval.

8 Del. C. ¬ß 228(e) - Stockholder Consent

Permits stockholders to take action by written consent without a meeting, provided the consent is signed by holders of outstanding stock having the minimum votes required. However, proper notice and disclosure requirements still apply to ensure all stockholders can make informed decisions.

8 Del. C. ¬ß 220(b) - Stockholder Inspection Rights

Grants stockholders the statutory right to inspect the corporation's books and records for any proper purpose reasonably related to their interest as stockholders. This right is particularly important when stockholders suspect wrongdoing, undisclosed conflicts, or improper conduct by management or the board.

Key Concepts: Corwin Cleansing & Disclosure

Corwin Cleansing: Under Corwin v. KKR Financial Holdings LLC, when a transaction not subject to the entire fairness standard is approved by a fully informed, uncoerced majority of disinterested stockholders, the business judgment rule applies and the transaction is presumed valid. However, this "cleansing" effect only applies if: (1) the vote was fully informed with proper disclosure of all material facts, (2) the majority was truly disinterested and uncoerced, and (3) no fraud or illegality occurred.

Disclosure Requirements: Delaware law requires that stockholders receive full and fair disclosure of all material facts before voting on significant transactions. Material facts include information that a reasonable stockholder would consider important in deciding how to vote, such as conflicts of interest, side compensation to insiders or key stockholders, financial projections, valuation opinions, and the fairness of the consideration. Failure to disclose material facts can invalidate the stockholder vote and eliminate Corwin cleansing protection.

Majority Vote and "Disinterested" Shares: For Corwin cleansing to apply, a majority of shares voting must be "disinterested"‚Äîmeaning held by stockholders who received no side benefits or special consideration beyond their pro rata share of the transaction proceeds. If certain stockholders received undisclosed compensation, employment agreements, or other benefits tied to their affirmative votes, their shares may be deemed "interested" and excluded from the cleansing calculation.

The ¬ß220 Books and Records Demand

Unable to obtain clear answers through informal requests, plaintiff stockholders filed this ¬ß220 action seeking to inspect Solfice's books and records. The goal is to investigate: (1) what compensation was provided to insiders and key stockholders, (2) whether all material facts were disclosed before the stockholder vote, (3) whether conflicts of interest were properly addressed, and (4) whether the transaction actually received approval from a majority of disinterested stockholders as required by Delaware law.

What This Means for Luminar Investors

Potential Issues

  • Disclosure Sufficiency: If material compensation was not disclosed before the stockholder vote, Luminar could face claims of inadequate disclosure or proxy fraud.
  • Related-Party/Inducement Questions: Payment to board members and key stockholders contingent on deal approval may constitute interested-party transactions requiring heightened scrutiny.
  • Rescissory/Disgorgement Exposure: If the transaction is found to violate ¬ß271 or fiduciary duties, remedies could include rescissory damages (return of consideration) or disgorgement of benefits.
  • Transaction Validity: Failure to obtain valid majority approval could render the asset acquisition voidable, creating uncertainty about ownership of acquired assets.

What Would Refute This

  • Clean Majority Math: Documentation showing that even after excluding all potentially interested votes, a majority of disinterested shares approved the transaction.
  • Uniform Disclosures: Evidence that all material facts, including all compensation to board members and key stockholders, were disclosed in proxy/consent materials before the vote.
  • Ordinary-Course Comp: Proof that employment agreements and RSUs were ordinary compensation not contingent on stockholder vote outcome.
  • Independent Oversight: Evidence of special committee review or other independent negotiation on behalf of disinterested stockholders.
